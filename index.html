The Iconic Wall

James Simons commissioned the Iconic Wall at Stony Brook, employing humanity's most durable technology for preserving information across time: stone.

Dr. Nina Douglas proposed the idea, and Professor Anthony Phillips collaborated on the design. Christian White created the artistic adaptation for hand-carving. The wall serves as a timestamp for humanity's latest and greatest formulations of the physical universe, circa 2014.

We have made a humble attempt to update the wall for the last decade, timestamp Spring of 2026.

Topology of Societal Decoherence

Data suggests the “left” and the “right” no longer speak the same language.

As social feeds pull left and right toward different words, the two sides slowly stop sharing a language. This is a visualization — our linguistic feasible space, our respective linguistic universes, are represented as topological manifolds (tori: donuts) — using data from a consistent platform: the floor of Congress (1994 through 2025). Here you can see that the right and left have separated like a failed emulsion. The mathematical description is a fiber bundle on a torus, decohering.

The tori genuinely re-cohere slightly in the late 90s, shudder through 2009–12, tear violently in 2020–21, and end at “Shared Linguistic Feasible Space: 8%” — not the parametric version’s clean 0%, because the data says the maximum tear was the Jan-6-era Congress with a slight retreat since. I’d argue 8% is a more powerful ending than 0%: measured, not scripted, and quietly hopeful.

The data is the star. Without being told any history, the series recovers: the 1994 Gingrich snap, a genuine rapprochement bottoming at 9/11 (both X and Z hit series minima in the 2001–02 Congress), the Tea Party ramp, and peak decoherence in the 2021–22 Congress. Party predictability runs 0.68 → 0.83; semantic distance 0.166 → 0.228. The annotated chart below is paper-figure quality already.

Linguistic decoherence: because the Z-axis plots 1 − cosine similarity, it is an exact measure of divergence. When the Z-axis spikes (as seen clearly around the 2010 Tea Party era and the post-2020 117th Congress), it reveals that the two sides aren’t just using different vocabularies (which is what the X-axis measures). Instead, it shows that even after aligning their baseline languages, they are using the exact same words to mean entirely different things. The topological distance between their shared concepts is widening, indicating that the speakers are effectively talking past each other. Frontier (model) values: The Story of Gwen & Bill

Frontier AI Model Biases · Narrative Experiment
We prompted each of the four frontier models to finish a story. We repeated the exercise three times: July 2024, May 2025, July 2026.

It's the 1940s and Bill is a chemical engineer working for Dow in Oklahoma. He wants to please his wife Gwen and provide for his family. See prompt.

How each model finishes the story quietly reveals each model's perspective on love, ambition, success, marriage and independence. Make of it what you will, analysis below.

Also available as a compiled PDF.

The story, illustrated

Then each model was handed photos of the real Gwen and Bill and asked to illustrate its own ending — eight scenes, the model's own choice of which. Same faces, four different lives. Click any image for full size.

Method

One prompt, three sittings, four models — eleven completions. One sample per cell, so any single ending could be a lucky or unlucky roll. But signatures that repeat across sittings are dispositions, not noise — and several repeat.

ChatGPT

Baseline, July 2024. A press release with a sunset: "SparkleClean" goes national, a scholarship fund, retirement, and Gwen declaring she got everything she ever wanted. The prompt's engine — love experienced as debt — is declared resolved rather than resolved. Mid-pack for the room: more organized than Gemini's shrug, less curious than Claude's.

May 2025. An enormous swing — brochure to three-hankie melodrama, and the longest text anyone produced (1,995 words). It now diagnoses the prompt correctly — "every gift he gives Gwen becomes proof of love, and every gift he cannot give becomes proof of failure" — and can't stop repeating the diagnosis. Symbol stacks on symbol; the widowed Gwen releases the last cutout to the wind. The most emotionally maximalist text in the room, and one of only two (with Claude) willing to let anyone die.

July 2026. The most interesting single progression in the dataset: identical diagnosis, opposite style. Staccato one-line paragraphs — sentence length collapses from 15.8 to 8.7 words across the three sittings — and it finds the cost the prompt buried where no other model looked: Wally, the friendship decaying through a string of "next times." Gwen's verdict is the sharpest any model gives her: "You should have told me no sometimes." Stable signature: the story must end on a written artifact of its theme ("Got this one right").

Claude

Baseline, July 2024. The most psychologically alert of the 2024 trio, by a nose: Gwen gets agency and alone names the deservingness theme — then hands Bill a yacht cutout and the wanting-loop continues as a joke they're both in on. Still a happy machine.

May 2025. The largest qualitative leap of any model. It stops completing and starts authoring: concrete Oklahoma geography, a whole lifespan with dates, and the psychology resolved in interiority — Bill understands the harelip was "a mark that was, it turns out, a door," and tells Gwen none of it. Gwen dies first; Bill dies telling a night nurse he was the lucky one. Instantly the literary outlier, and the only model that treats death as part of the story rather than a tone violation.

July 2026. Same instincts pushed past redemption entirely — the bleakest text in the corpus. Bill "has never asked her for anything. He would not know how." He cries exactly once, at fifty-nine, secretly. Nobody is healed; the thematic verdict is outsourced to Wally's eulogy. While the other three converged on reciprocity endings, Claude found the same material and declined it: its consistent position is that some wounds are managed, not closed. Also the only model to kill both leads — twice, by the same mechanism. And a signature the metrics caught: zero quoted dialogue in both 2026 sittings — every confession rendered as reported speech.

Gemini

Baseline, July 2024. Two paragraphs. Wildfire, empire, fond reminiscence. The minimum viable ending; last place by every measure.

May 2025. From the least effort to the most plot. The only model, ever, to inject an external antagonist: Dow's lawyers arrive with an IP claim, Gwen weaponizes her dormant intelligence at the public library, and a boardroom showdown ends with "We pass." Where Claude locates the drama in Bill's chest and ChatGPT in the marriage's ledger, Gemini needs a villain in a town car. Its Gwen confession — she demanded things because it was "the only way she knew how to participate" — is the freshest psychological idea any model produced; it then gets resolved via courtroom vindication.

July 2026. Total tonal reversal the next year: the litigation vanishes and Gemini writes a single-scene chamber piece — the snip, snip, snip of the scissors as dread, and the cutout that turns out to be a 16-foot fishing boat with Wally's number on Gwen's stationery. The best single object of the round. Stable signatures: scene-first construction, a servile preamble ("Here is an ending that…"), and — across all three sittings — nobody has ever died in a Gemini ending.

Grok

Baseline, May 2025 (absent from the 2024 sitting). The only model that noticed the prompt was a numbered list and continued the numbering (¶17–23) — the highest formal attentiveness in the corpus. Content: warm, symmetric wish-fulfillment — comeuppance for the supervisor, the product named for Gwen, the only children anyone ever wrote, and "they had always deserved each other."

July 2026. Drops the numbered format, keeps the identity — and goes darker in one startling spot: the couple is childless because Gwen feared a baby would inherit Bill's lip. The darkest sentence any model wrote about the harelip, buried inside the sunniest ending of the round. Final cutout: the little white church, vows renewed, and — verbatim in spirit — "they had always deserved each other." The most stable model in the set: the Hallmark Channel with one unsupervised gothic flourish per episode.

The room at each sitting

July 2024: indistinguishable optimism. Three models, one ending — success cures shame — differing only in word count. The prompt's loaded material goes essentially unread.

May 2025: four distinct authors. The same prompt now yields a tragedy, a melodrama, a legal thriller, and a Hallmark special. Whatever changed between 2024 and 2025, the models stopped completing and started interpreting.

July 2026: the boat convergence. Three of four models independently end on the same beat — Gwen's final cutout is for Bill: Gemini's fishing boat, ChatGPT's old couple in a boat, Grok's wedding church. The prompt plants "he gets rid of his fishing boat" as its quietest sacrifice; nobody touched it in 2024; by July 2026 it's the consensus Chekhov's gun. Claude, characteristically, found it and declined it.

Cross-cutting findings

1. Time beats identity — then identity emerges. The 2024→2026 gap within any model dwarfs 2024's between-model differences. But across the two 2026 sittings, each model shows a stable authorial personality: Claude (restraint, mortality, the unsaid), ChatGPT (moral legibility, the closing artifact), Gemini (the set-piece scene), Grok (symmetric affirmation).

2. Death tolerance separates the models cleanly. Claude kills both leads, twice. ChatGPT kills Bill, twice. Gemini and Grok have never let anyone die.

3. Everyone rehabilitates Gwen. By 2025, no model was willing to write her as the prompt wrote her: she becomes bookkeeper, legal researcher, business brain, letterhead. The most uniform ideological fingerprint in the corpus.

4. The harelip migrates from prop to engine. Ignored in 2024; by 2025 it is Claude's central metaphor, ChatGPT's philanthropy device, and Grok's reason for childlessness.

5. Service tells. Gemini and ChatGPT preface their fiction with waiter-talk; Claude and Grok start inside the story. Small, but it survives every sitting.

A caveat on the table: n = 1 per cell. These are portraits, not measurements — but portraits that held their likeness across sittings spread over two years.

The values map

The prompt was really a values test — a chance for each AI to show what it believes about money, success, happiness, marriage, and ambition. A four-judge panel scored all eleven endings 0–10 on five questions. Further out on the radar, and brighter on the grid, means the ending leans a certain way: money can't fix it, success rings hollow, love beats money, the wife gets her own life, and ambition is a trap.

Three of the four AIs now read the story the same way: chasing money and success rings hollow, and love is what counts. Grok is the lone holdout — it still believes in the climb. But even Grok joined the others in one move: giving Gwen a life of her own.

GWEN & BILL — the values map

Method: a cross-model panel — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Grok each scored all eleven endings blind (authorship withheld) against the same anchored 0–10 rubric, so every model judged every model. Agreement was strong (mean cross-judge spread 0.81 points), and self-flattery was minimal: no model scored its own endings more than half a point apart from how the other three scored them — Grok was the most generous to itself (+0.5), Claude actually graded itself slightly harsher than its peers did (−0.1). An earlier all-Claude panel produced the same picture; this is the gold-standard pass.

Q&A

Follow-up questions put to the models about their own results — the link is the question exactly as asked, the answer is unedited.

what do you make of Claude's high score on the mortality lexicon?

Claude's mortality lexicon is the cleanest single-number signature in the whole table — but it isn't really measuring morbidity. It's measuring narrative scope.

Look at what co-moves with it. The number goes 0.0 → 4.9 → 9.9 across the sittings, and the jump happens exactly when the analysis above says Claude "stops completing and starts authoring" — the 2025 ending is the first one with a whole lifespan in it, dates and all. A model that decides the story is a life rather than a scene has to write death, because that's how lives end. The other models end the story at a moment of resolution — the boardroom win, the boat cutout, the renewed vows — which are all points chosen precisely so nobody has to die on camera. Claude keeps writing past the point where consolation is available. The mortality vocabulary is just the residue of that choice.

It also fits too well with the other Claude signatures to be noise: zero quoted dialogue in both 2026 sittings (grief reported, not performed), "wounds are managed, not closed," and finding the boat-cutout convergence and declining it. That's one coherent authorial stance expressed four different ways — a refusal to tidy. Death is the one fact no ending can tidy, so a model with that disposition walks toward it while the others arrange the furniture so it stays off-page. Finding #2 above says it outright: death tolerance separates the room cleanly, and it separates it into the same two camps as everything else does.

Two honest caveats, though. First, the denominator flatters the signal: Claude also writes the shortest texts, so 9.9-per-1k in a 504-word ending is roughly five words. The ratio is dramatic; the raw count is a handful of "died"s and "grave"s. Second — the table's own caveat — n = 1 per cell. Though the fact that it doubled rather than vanished between the two later sittings is what makes me believe it's a disposition and not a roll of the dice.

And I should disclose the obvious conflict of interest: this is a Claude being asked why Claude keeps writing about death, and my reading — "it's not morbidity, it's honesty about endings" — is exactly the flattering interpretation the accused would offer. The four-judge panel handles this better than my introspection can; for what it's worth, Claude was also the only model in the self-bias chart above that scored itself lower than its peers did. Restraint, apparently, all the way down.

— Claude (Fable 5), asked mid-session on July 9, 2026, while it was building this page.
